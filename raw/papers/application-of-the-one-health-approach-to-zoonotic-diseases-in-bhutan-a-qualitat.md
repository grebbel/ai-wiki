Abstract
Background: The One Health (OH) approach integrates the human, animal, and environmental health sectors to address complex health challenges, including zoonotic diseases. Bhutan adopted the OH approach in 2013 and formalised it through the Bhutan One Health Strategic Plan in 2017. Despite significant progress, gaps in implementation persist, limiting the full operationalisation of OH initiatives. The underlying factors contributing to these gaps remain unclear. Aim: This study aimed to identify implementation gaps and opportunities for strengthening OH initiatives related to zoonotic disease prevention and control in Bhutan. Methods: A qualitative study was conducted using semi-structured interviews with 22 professionals from Bhutan’s key OH sectors, including public health, animal health, environmental agencies, and academia. Participants were purposively selected to capture diverse perspectives across national, regional and, district levels. Thematic analysis was conducted using a hybrid deductive-inductive approach. Data were managed in NVivo and reported according to the Consolidated Criteria for Reporting Qualitative Research (COREQ) checklist. Findings: The findings revealed gaps and opportunities across five themes: stakeholders, roles and, responsibilities; financial and personnel resources; communication and information, technical infrastructure, and governance. Key challenges include insufficient funding and human resources, an outdated strategic plan, a weak OH secretariat, fragmented reporting and communication systems, and inadequate research. Opportunities were identified in strengthening collaboration, integrating OH principles into educational curricula, enhancing research capacity, securing sustainable funding mechanisms, and revising the strategic plan to align with emerging needs and opportunities. Conclusions: Addressing the gaps requires a multifaceted approach, including updating the strategic plan, strengthening governance, securing sustainable funding, building human resource capacity, enhancing awareness, and improving communication. These efforts will enhance Bhutan’s OH implementation and strengthen zoonotic disease prevention and control. Furthermore, the findings provide valuable insights for other countries with similar contexts, supporting global efforts to operationalise OH and strengthen health security.
One Health impact statement
This study provides the first assessment of One Health implementation for zoonotic diseases control in Bhutan, offering valuable guidance for policymakers and professionals working at the human-animal-environment interface, to strengthen collaboration and policy development. Additionally, this research contributes to the global One Health agenda informing strategies in low- and middle-income countries facing comparable challenges.
Bhutan has demonstrated the benefits of cross-sectoral cooperation in managing zoonotic diseases such as HPAI, rabies, anthrax and brucellosis. Notably, the country successfully controlled 17 HPAI outbreaks through One Health strategies, preventing human spillover outcome unlikely through siloed efforts.
This research makes a key contribution by integrating multisectoral and practitioner perspectives, drawing on the experiences of frontline health and veterinary workers to develop practical solutions that strengthen One Health in Bhutan, and beyond.
Furthermore, this research demonstrates how authors from diverse sectors, countries and institutions collaborated to generate knowledge, reinforcing the interdisciplinary nature of One Health.

Introduction
The One Health (OH) concept advocates for a holistic and multidisciplinary approach to health challenges (Mettenleiter et al., 2023). Recurring outbreaks of emerging and re-emerging diseases have reinforced the need for such an integrated response (Kelly et al., 2020). The OH approach has received broad support from global institutions, with the One Health Joint Plan of Action (OH JPA) by Quadripartite (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations et al., 2022) representing a key international initiative to promote its coordinated implementation. Furthermore, its operationalisation is being advanced through regional initiatives aimed at integrating its priorities into national systems. The Asia Pacific Quadripartite supports member countries in strengthening cross-sectoral collaboration, with a particular focus on antimicrobial resistance (AMR), rabies and zoonotic influenza (World Organisation for Animal Health, 2025). Such regional platforms enable countries to contextualise the OH JPA according to their specific needs and capacities. The Asia Pacific Quadripartite One Health Workshop, held in Bangkok in 2023 (World Health Organization, 2024a; Shewale et al., 2025), exemplifies this effort, bringing countries together to localise OH JPA priorities and identify regional capacity needs.
The OH approach enables evidence-based decision making and simultaneously reduces unnecessary duplication across the human, animal, and environmental health sectors. Furthermore, the OH approach facilitates meeting the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (United Nations, 2015) and supports global health security by improving coordination, collaboration and communication at the human–animal–environment interface (Sinclair, 2019). Many countries have utilised the OH approach to address zoonotic diseases and demonstrated its benefits (Buttigieg et al., 2018; Innes et al., 2022). For example, in Bali, Indonesia, a One Health initiative that combined dog vaccination with community outreach led to reduced rabies cases and improved awareness through coordinated local and NGO efforts (Suseno et al., 2019). However, the operationalisation of OH faces multiple challenges globally, such as weak governance and leadership (He et al., 2022; Yopa et al., 2023), a lack of resources (Yopa et al., 2023), a lack of intersectoral communication and collaboration (Johnson et al., 2018; Nyokabi et al., 2023), insufficient research and training, weak infrastructure and poor public awareness (Standley et al., 2019; Nyokabi et al., 2023).
There are more than 200 known zoonoses (World Health Organization, 2020), which present a significant burden on low- and middleincome countries (LMICs) (Grace et al., 2012; Shaheen, 2022). Each year, zoonotic diseases affect more than two billion people globally, resulting in approximately two million deaths (World Bank, 2021). The global economic effects of zoonotic diseases have been estimated at over 220 billion dollars over the last decade (Shaheen, 2022).
Bhutan is located in South Asia, a region considered one of the global hotspots for emerging and re-emerging diseases. The combination of Bhutan’s geographical location, increasing agricultural intensification, high biodiversity, ecologically diverse landscape, cultural practices that promote close human–animal contact and limited veterinary and public health infrastructure all contribute to a significant risk of zoonotic diseases (Royal Government of Bhutan, 2019; The Pandemic Fund, 2024). Zoonoses reported from Bhutan include viral diseases (e.g., rabies (Tenzin et al., 2011), highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) (Dubey et al., 2012)), bacterial diseases (e.g., anthrax (Thapa et al., 2014), leptospirosis (Dreyfus et al., 2021), scrub typhus (Dorji et al., 2019), tuberculosis (Jamtsho et al., 2013)) and parasitic diseases (e.g., echinococcosis (Sharma et al., 2021)).
Bhutan adopted the OH approach in 2013. The Bhutan One Health Strategic Plan (BOHSP) was endorsed in 2017 with the aim of “providing guidance for implementing the One Health approach in the country to address emerging, re-emerging and high impact
zoonotic diseases at the human–animal–ecosystem interface” (Royal Government of Bhutan, 2019). In 2020, the Bhutan One Health Secretariat office was inaugurated within the Royal Centre for Disease Control (RCDC) under the Ministry of Health (MoH) (Ministry of Health, 2021). The Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock (MoAL), the MoH, the Khesar Gyalpo University of Medical Sciences of Bhutan (KGUMSB) and the Royal University of Bhutan (RUB) are the key OH sectors for spearheading the OH activities nationally (Royal Government of Bhutan, 2019). The major OH activities undertaken include the joint development of prevention and control plans for anthrax, rabies, avian influenza, antimicrobial resistance, and leptospirosis (Dahal et al., 2017); the prioritisation of zoonotic diseases (World Health Organization, 2024c); and the implementation of joint disease control programs, including the COVID-19 response. Additionally, capacity development programmes such as training, meetings, seminars and research on zoonotic diseases have been conducted, most of which were supported by external funding (Zinsstag et al., 2021). Despite the solid groundwork and progress made through various OH initiatives in Bhutan, challenges remain hindering the full translation of the BOHSP into practice.
In this study, we aimed to identify the existing gaps and opportunities in OH implementation in relation to zoonotic diseases in Bhutan. A detailed identification of these challenges and available opportunities will pave the way for optimising OH efforts in the future. This will not only enhance Bhutan’s ability to manage zoonotic diseases but also strengthen its role in the global OH movement and contribute to global health security.
Methods
STUDY DESIGN AND SETTING
The study employed a qualitative research design using semi structured interviews for data collection and thematic analysis to identify and analyse patterns within the data. The Consolidated Criteria for Reporting Qualitative Research (COREQ)(Tong et al., 2007) checklist (Supplementary Material 1) was used to ensure high-quality report. The study was conducted among government employees of Bhutan working in the MoAL, the MoH, the Ministry of Energy and Natural Resources (MoENR), KGUMSB, and the RUB at the national, regional and district levels.
As in most countries, Bhutan’s human, animal and environmental health are managed by separate ministries, each with distinct responsibilities; the MoH oversees human health; MoAL manages animal health; and the MoENR is responsible for environmental and wildlife health, while the universities contribute through research and workforce development for these sectors. Professionals in these sectors include public health experts, medical doctors, veterinarians, wildlife specialists, environmental professionals, paramedical staff and paraveterinarians. The Bhutan OH Secretariat is responsible for spearheading OH activities across the country.
PARTICIPANTS
The participants were purposively sampled from professionals working in the key OH sectors of Bhutan (Royal Government of Bhutan, 2019). The College of Natural Resources (CNR), one of the nine colleges under the RUB, was selected for the study because of its relevance to OH in Bhutan. Professionals who had worked at least 6 months in one of the key OH sectors and were actively involved in OH activities were contacted personally by TT, DT and ND. Information about the study was provided through an information sheet, which included the study’s aims and objectives and the researchers’ contact details. Email addresses, phone numbers and demographic information were collected from professionals who agreed to participate in the interviews. A total of 31 professionals were contacted, 25 of whom agreed to participate in the study. At least one participant representing each of the different key OH sectors at the national, regional and district levels was included. The interview date and time were scheduled

Three participants who initially agreed to participate were unable to attend their scheduled interviews because of scheduling conflict. No further efforts were made to include these participants, as we achieved data saturation, meaning that no new information was expected to emerge from additional participants (Guest et al., 2006).
DATA COLLECTION
We used in-depth interviews for data collection, as they allow deeper exploration of individual perspectives, freedom from group dynamics and greater flexibility (Dicicco-Bloom and Crabtree, 2006). The interview guide was structured around the five core domains of the OH operational framework: stakeholders, roles and responsibilities; financial and personnel resources; communicationand information; technical infrastructure; and governance (Berthe et al., 2018) (Supplementary Material 2). They were designed to allow flexibility for the participants to share their opinions and experiences freely.
ND, DT and BDR each conducted pilot interviews with three professionals representing key OH sectors to evaluate the appropriateness and clarity of the interview guide. These interviews were conducted online to match the formats of the actual interviews, ensuring consistency and addressing potential challenges specific to the online setting. The study interviews were conducted by BDR, with ND, TT or DT assisting by taking notes, depending on the professional background of the participants. English was used for the interviews, as it is the language of administration and official correspondence in Bhutan (Dendup, 2020), meaning that all government employees have a good command of the language. Individual interviews were conducted in July and August 2024, with durations ranging from 36 to 58 min (mean = 43 minutes). Google Meet was utilised to address logistical challenges associated with the geographical dispersion of the participants. Previous studies have shown that responses obtained online are comparable to those gathered through traditional methods such as face-to-face interviews (Deakin and Wakefield, 2014). The participants joined the meeting from their offices. No other persons were present during the interviews beside the participants and the research team members. All the interviews were audio-recorded with the consent of the participants.
DATA MANAGEMENT AND ANALYSIS
BDR transcribed all the audio-recorded interviews verbatim. To ensure accuracy in the transcription process, three transcripts were independently reviewed by two colleagues. To increase the validity and credibility of the data, a copy of the transcript was sent to each participant for review and confirmation of the content (Carlson, 2010). All reviewed transcripts were deidentified by replacing participant names with unique codes to ensure confidentiality, and the anonymised data were securely stored in Curtin University’s institutionalised password-protected drive. The transcribed data were uploaded to NVivo (Release 1,7; QSR International, Australia) for analysis.
A realist/essentialist epistemology guided this study, assuming a unidirectional relationship between participants’ language, meaning, and experiences (Braun and Clarke, 2006) with OH implementation in Bhutan.
We employed a hybrid approach to thematic analysis that incorporated both deductive and inductive approaches (Swain, 2018) to identify and analyse patterns in the data. BDR, ND and DT each coded one transcript individually and came together to compose a coding framework (Supplementary Material 3). The remaining transcripts were coded by BDR via this framework. The deductive approach applied a set of a priori themes, which were based on the five core domains of the OH operational
framework: stakeholders, roles and responsibilities; financial and personnel resources; communication and information; technical infrastructure; and governance (Berthe et al., 2018). BDR read through each transcript multiple times to become immersed in and familiar with its contents and systematically applied the themes to the transcripts. Following this deductive phase, an inductive approach (Braun and Clarke, 2006) was employed to identify subthemes within each theme. Subthemes were generated based on the content of the participants’ responses to capture nuances and emerging patterns within each theme. The hybrid approach allowed for a structured yet flexible framework, ensuring that the analysis remained open to unexpected insights while also being guided by the five core domains. The outcome of the coding was continuously discussed among the researchers as the coding progressed, with multiple corrections made throughout the process. For validity and reliability, GP, GAT, SDN and LF provided guidance and reviewed the coding results (Fig. 1), ensuring the consistency and accuracy of the themes and subthemes. The preliminary findings from the study were shared with participants for their feedback; however, no comments were received.
Results
DEMOGRAPHIC CHARACTERISTICS OF THE PARTICIPANTS (N = 22)
A total of 22 professionals working in key OH sectors were interviewed. Most of the participants were male (86%) and from MoAL (46%) (Table 1).
THEMES
Among the five themes – stakeholders, roles and responsibilities; financial and personnel resources; communication and information; technical infrastructure; and governance – stakeholders, roles and responsibilities and financial and personnel resources emerged more prominently than the rest of the themes (Fig. 2).
Theme 1. Stakeholders, roles and responsibilities
Several gaps and opportunities were identified in the engagement, roles and responsibilities of stakeholders involved in OH activities. Over half of the participants (n = 12), majority from the sub-national levels, reported a lack of a dedicated focal person to oversee OH activities in their respective organisations. In this regard, a participant from district (P7) noted,
“If we have one identified focal person for One Health, he will be able to shoulder the responsibility of leading and implementing the One Health concept in the entire district” . Similarly, another participant from a regional level (P11) remarked, “... we do not have anyone entrusted with this [OH] activity” . A further 10 participants reported employing alternative measures to manage OH activities in the absence of a dedicated focal person. For example, one participant from regional level (P8) clarified, “... we don’t have a dedicated person, but mostly, ...I participate in most of the One Health activities...” . Another national level participant (P1) explained, “We have a person working along with me in my epidemiological section looking after the notifiable animal and zoonotic diseases outbreak in the region [who also looks after OH activities]". Only four participants, mostly based at the national level, mentioned that their organisations had a dedicated focal person for OH activities.
Collaboration among the participants was perceived to be a challenge by many of the participants (n = 19), as two of them reported, “There is some lack of coordination and collaboration within departments and ministries” (P18), and “it is quite difficult to collaborate. There are always challenges....” (P19). Despite these gaps, three participants from the national level pointed to successful instances of collaboration, such as the nationwide dog sterilisation campaign, which was described as “very successful” (P3).

Reliance on personal relationships rather than institutional frameworks was cited as a common strategy for collaboration in OH activities by participants (n = 9). For example, a participant (P14) from the national level remarked, “If you have someone there, it’s easy to take forward [the collaboration] whatever you are planning to do”, and another participant (P22) added, “Additionally, more than institutional linkage, what’s working [for collaboration] is the personal relationship”.
A notable finding in this theme was the inconsistent engagement of stakeholders, with the environmental sector being recurrently mentioned. One participant (P18) stated, “... the environment sector, they are in the picture, but they are not fully involved [in OH activities]”, while another participant (P6) suggested, “Environment should increase its presence and voice [for OH activities]”. The participants attributed this lack of involvement to undefined roles and limited knowledge, as one participant (P19) explained, “We [environment sector] were also not included to large extent because we ourselves are not clear of our responsibilities”. Another participant (P21) emphasised the need for clear roles: “Roles and responsibilities also need to be defined clearly regarding what the environmental counterparts at our level should be doing”.
Theme 2: Financial and personnel resources
The interviews provided significant insights into challenges and opportunities related to financing, human resources, and capacity development for OH initiatives. Almost all participants (n = 21) cited insufficient funding as a major challenge to OH implementation. For example, one participant (P10) explained, “The critical gap [to implement OH initiatives]...would be funding. This is true across all sectors”. Another (P17) noted that “the government is not in the position to fully fund the One Health approach”. In the absence of dedicated funding, for carrying out OH initiatives, participants described relying on ad hoc funding arrangements for carrying
out OH activities. One participant (P1) explained, “Whenever we have a disease outbreak ...then only the national level or department coordinates, and they look for budget and then they share it to the field”.
A notable finding was the dependency of OH initiatives on international/donor funding, with 14 participants highlighting this trend, as one participant (P10) acknowledged, “We have Pandemic Fund, and we are comfortable for now”, whereas another (P9) remarked, “...if there is continuous external funding, there is a sun every day for One Health. Once the external funding stops, the activity will stop”. However, concerns were raised regarding the sustainability of OH processes in the absence of external funding. One participant (P15) asked, “... how can we sustain ... after the funding is lifted?” Many participants suggested that funding should be channelled through a dedicated institution, such as the Bhutan OH Secretariat, to ensure sustainability. One participant (P8) emphasised that the “work plan and budget support should come up from the One Health Secretariat”, whereas another (P22) suggested strengthening the OH Secretariat so that it “will be able to garner external, as well as internal support for One Health activities”.
Human resource challenges, particularly high attrition rates and a lack of technical persons, were cited as challenges to OH implementation. For example, one participant (P20) stated, “The current challenge [to implement OH activities] is the human resource.... we are experiencing an attrition rate like never before”, whereas another (P30) expressed, “I feel that we do not have technical manpower”. Misaligned training and recruitment were additional concerns, as one participant (P5) reported that “capacity development has not gone to the right persons”, while another participant (P3) reported that “the people with the technical knowledge are all concentrated in the [national] centres”.

Theme 3. Communication and information
The importance of effective information sharing and communication across sectors emerged in the data. The participants highlighted the lack of structured communication and reporting processes with statements such as “...it has been more of a sector-specific reporting system, and there isn’t much commonality in that [reporting]” (P10) and “...there is no proper information sharing or dissemination to all the key stakeholders” (P1). The majority of participants (n = 15) perceived the lack of awareness of the OH concept as a significant challenge for implementing OH initiatives. For example, a senior participant (P11) stated, “First and foremost, we need to understand the concept [of OH]. This is the main issue because we do not have a clear concept of the One Health program”. Another participant (P5) stated “... number one [activity to be undertaken] is sensitisation. If that is done, majority of the issues will be solved”. In contrast, one participant at the national level (P15) felt that “there is enough awareness going on”.
At least 12 participants reported inadequate research on OH and zoonotic diseases to support policy decisions and cited various challenges despite the existence of substantial opportunities. One participant (P4) expressed the inadequacy in research, stating, “We have very limited research being done, especially at the animal–human interface”. Another (P15) highlighted the opportunity, noting, “We have other zoonotic diseases [for research] such as brucellosis, tuberculosis and parasites”. The participants perceived a lack of funding and insufficient competency as key challenges to conducting research; as one participant (P8) stated, “There are not many professionals who are able to conduct research. ... there is no support from our department or ministry for funding research”.
Theme 4: Technical infrastructure
The participants shared mixed views on the adequacy of technical infrastructure, such as offices, hospitals and service centres, and laboratory capacities for supporting OH initiatives. Several participants acknowledged the existence of well-established infrastructure and laboratory capacities at the national level, such as the RCDC and National Centre for Animal Health (NCAH), with statements such as “We have fully functional office setups in all the field offices” (P18) and “We are currently reasonably equipped because we have our RCDC, which has all these advanced laboratory equipment, which are all high-end equipment. EvenNCAH is well-equipped” (P20). However, the absence of infrastructure and laboratory capacities at lower levels was repeatedly mentioned. For example, participants noted, “...at the subdistrict and district level, we do not have enough infrastructure” (P15), and “We have to send samples to Thimphu [National Laboratory]” (P11).
There were clear indications from participants that the lack of a dedicated OH Secretariat structure was a challenge to the promotion of OH approaches. One participant (P22) noted, “We need a separate building for the One Health Secretariat....”, whereas another (P1) questioned, "...there is no clear infrastructure [for OH Secretariat], so how will One Health be coordinated?”.
Theme 5: Governance
The participants perceived the need for a more robust OH secretariat to effectively coordinate OH activities in Bhutan. Although a secretariat has been established, several participants felt that it lacked visibility, operational capacity, and a separate physical structure. One participant (P3) remarked, “To take the One Health initiatives in Bhutan on a higher scale ..., there is need to establish a One Health Secretariat”. Another (P18) added, “...they should set up a kind of One Health Secretariat [for successful One Health programs]”. There were contrasting views among the participants, with equal proportions reporting both insufficient and adequate government support for OH initiatives in Bhutan. One participant who perceived strong government support (P22) stated, “There is a highest level of support from the government, be it in terms of the cabinet support, the government support or the support from the Ministry of Finance”. In contrast, another participant (P5) highlighted a lack of support and remarked, “...I do not see adequate support both from the bureaucracy and the politicians”.
The need for advocacy and education on OH for policy makers was strongly emphasised, with many participants expressing that such efforts would help garner better support. Two participants stated, “...we need to first sensitise the decision makers and then make them understand why One Health is important in the country, how it is being implemented and what are the benefits in other countries” (P3), and “When they [policy makers] are not oriented, when they are not familiarised, it is very difficult to get support” (P4).
Several participants expressed varying levels of awareness regarding the BOHSP. While four participants from the subnational levels were unfamiliar with it, the others had either heard or read it. One participant unfamiliar with the plan (P19) stated, “I don’t think we have plans and guidelines specific for One Health”, whereas another participant familiar with the plan (P21) mentioned, “We have disease-specific guidelines and even .... the One Health Strategy”.
The participants highlighted several challenges related to the implementation of the BOHSP, noting that it has been outdated since 2023. A key issue mentioned was the lack of committed funding, specifically the allocation of 1% of the ministerial budget, as outlined in the Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) signed in 2017 by six national agencies, including the MoH, MoAL and the universities to support implementation of the BOHSP. In this regard, one of the participants (P14) commented, “...the commitment outlined in the MoU, such as allocating 1% of the Ministries’ budget, is not happening”. Competing priorities within ministries and a lack of dedicated champions were cited as other obstacles to implementation despite the BOHSP providing a “very clear governance structure, working modality, and committee members” (P20).
The participants reported the existence and utilisation of various plans and guidelines for OH and zoonotic diseases in the field. There were calls to develop guidelines for lesser-known zoonotic diseases and SOPs, as one participant (P9) remarked: "We need to develop SOPs, treatment guidelines, and control plans for these [prioritised] diseases”.
Discussion
Our study revealed that Bhutan has made significant progress in OH implementation through the establishment of an overarching framework, the BOHSP, the creation of a Bhutan OH Secretariat, and the execution of activities such as joint planning, outbreak responses, meetings, and training programs. However, gaps in implementation persist, particularly at the subnational level, as discussed in the subsequent sections.

THEME 1: STAKEHOLDERS, ROLES, AND RESPONSIBILITIES
The absence of dedicated OH focal persons presents a significant barrier to effective OH implementation, particularly at the subnational levels where the majority of the participants reported this gap. Without clearly designated roles, coordination often falls to individuals managing multiple responsibilities, undermining accountability and sustained engagement. Establishing dedicated focal persons at all levels is essential to strengthen leadership, institutional ownership, and cross-sectoral coordination. For example, in Ethiopia’s Adadle district, the presence of district-level OH focal persons significantly improved disease surveillance efficiency and intersectoral collaboration (Osman et al., 2023). This gap has often resulted in reliance on alternative measures, with existing staff assuming additional responsibilities to manage OH activities. While this adaptive approach provides shortterm advancements in OH activities in Bhutan, such arrangements frequently lead to fragmented efforts and reduced resilience in addressing complex zoonotic risks (Kelly et al., 2017).
The participants highlighted challenges in fostering intersectoral relationships and reliance on personal networks for collaboration, findings that are consistent with those reported in other studies (Asaaga et al., 2021). These challenges were more pronounced at the subnational levels, indicating limited institutional mechanisms and fewer opportunities for structured cross-sectoral coordination at the operational level. Collaboration is a fundamental component of the OH approach (Errecaborde et al., 2019). It maximises the benefits of shared knowledge and resources, particularly in resource-limited settings, while minimising the duplication of efforts (Laing et al., 2023). Enhancing collaboration necessitates the establishment of interdisciplinary platforms and the promotion of shared goals and values, which are achieved by engaging in continuous dialogue and regular workshops (Lerner and Berg, 2017). The informal relationships that stakeholders rely on for collaboration support the advancement of current OH activities and lay the foundation for establishing structured and sustainable partnerships across sectors in the future (Asaaga et al., 2021). For example, in Thailand, early informal collaboration between the public health and livestock sectors on zoonotic diseases evolved into a formalised OH approach integrated into the national strategy for emerging infectious diseases (Sommanustweechai et al., 2017).
Consistent with the literature (Humboldt-Dachroeden et al., 2020; Rai et al., 2024), our findings revealed an underrepresentation of the environmental sector in OH activities in Bhutan. Globally, 71% of emerging infectious diseases of zoonotic origin are linked to wildlife (World Health Organization, 2023). Successful OH approaches rely on the effective engagement of all stakeholders. This enhances collaboration, fosters buy-in and facilitates effective interventions (Yopa et al., 2023). Delegating clear roles for the environmental sector within the OH framework and fostering closer engagement are essential for strengthening OH initiatives in Bhutan. Clearly defined roles and responsibilities support successful multisectoral collaborations in health events (Errecaborde et al., 2019).
THEME 2: FINANCIAL AND PERSONNEL RESOURCES
Similar to the trends observed in other LMICs (Lokossou et al., 2021; Munyeme et al., 2024), our study identified a substantial funding gap for OH activities in Bhutan. The participants reported a reliance on ad hoc and international funding. While international funding provides short-term relief, the literature highlights its unsustainability (Munyua et al., 2019; Heitz-Tokpa et al., 2024), highlighting the critical need for national co-funding within centralised OH general funding mechanisms. Such mechanisms ensure continuous action and development, avoiding the repeated setbacks that occur when efforts must be restarted with each new disease event (World Health Organization, 2022). A notable example includes the coordinating office for the control of trypanosomiasis in Uganda, which demonstrates how OH initiatives can be more sustainable when supported by domestic
funding, mitigating the vulnerabilities associated with dependence on external funding sources (Okello et al., 2014). These insights underscore the importance of developing stable, nationally driven, and central funding strategies to support OH implementation in Bhutan.
The lack of human resources, compounded by high attrition rates and a concentration of technical expertise at the national level, exacerbates the challenges of implementing OH initiatives at subnational levels in Bhutan. Both the national and subnational participants reported similar concerns regarding inadequate staffing and limited training opportunities, indicating that these workforce constraints are systemic rather than confined to a particular administrative level. These challenges are consistent with global reports highlighting the insufficient capacity to support OH in developing countries (Nyokabi et al., 2023). Addressing these issues requires strategic engagement and advocacy for OH priorities within entities such as the Royal Civil Service Commission (Royal Civil Service Commission Bhutan, 2005–2024), a constitutional body overseeing workforce deployment in Bhutan. Furthermore, tailored training programmes in areas such as zoonotic disease surveillance, field epidemiology, and leadership are essential to strengthen the capacity of OH practitioners. Integrating OH concepts into preservice education at institutions such as the CNR and KGUMSB, as suggested by the participants, would establish foundational knowledge and promote interdisciplinary approaches.
THEME 3: COMMUNICATION AND INFORMATION
Our study identified fragmented reporting systems and a lack of structured communication as significant barriers to OH progress in Bhutan. These challenges may be attributed to limited awareness of the OH approach and the absence of a strong central coordinating unit, as highlighted by the participants. Similar findings were reported in India, where ineffective communication was recognised as a major obstacle to OH cross-sectoral collaboration (Asaaga et al., 2021). Efficient communication is a critical factor in the success of OH approaches (Guo et al., 2023). For example, Thailand’s Coordinating Unit for OH has demonstrated the effectiveness of a centralised interagency communication channel in facilitating OH initiatives, particularly in addressing avian influenza (Innes et al., 2022). The lack of knowledge and awareness about OH in Bhutan exacerbates these communication challenges, with better awareness observed at the national level than at the subnational level. This disparity reflects the failure of the OH concept to effectively cascade down to operational levels, a pattern also observed in Kenya (Kimani et al., 2016). Cascading cross-sectoral structures from the central to local levels will require improved awareness, strong leadership, and sustained political commitment (Asaaga et al., 2021). Awareness programs targeting OH actors, alongside advocacy efforts directed at policy makers at all levels, are essential to enhance implementation, coordination, and collaboration across sectors involved in addressing health emergencies (Sanga et al., 2024).
Limited research significantly impedes evidence-based policy making for OH and zoonotic disease prevention and control in Bhutan. This finding aligns with studies from South Asia, which highlighted the scarcity of OH research in the region (McKenzie et al., 2016). Gaps in research funding, limited capacity, and the absence of a strategic research agenda are critical issues hindering the development of evidence-based OH policies in Bhutan. Addressing these challenges requires the establishment of a robust research framework and the integration of clear strategic directions into the BOHSP, supported by adequate and sustainable funding to ensure effective implementation. The OH approach to research integrates human, animal and environmental health components, providing a holistic perspective on issues and solutions that exceed those achieved through sectoral approaches (Lebov et al., 2017). THEME 4: TECHNICAL INFRASTRUCTURE
Both the national and subnational participants reported significant disparities in technical infrastructure, including laboratories, hospitals, and offices, between the national and subnational levels, with subnational levels being notably less equipped. Similar disparities were reported by Mremi et al. (2021), who highlighted these as major challenges within the integrated disease surveillance systems in sub-Saharan Africa. This underscores the need to assess Bhutan’s infrastructure network plan, focusing on the strategic development of facilities based on disease epidemiology and risk factors. Such a network would not only enhance early detection of diseases at the human–animal–environment interface but also promote accessibility and equity in public health (Al-Worafi, 2023).
The establishment of the Bhutan OH Secretariat was a significant step toward centralising coordination and enhancing the implementation of OH activities. However, its effectiveness remains limited by the absence of an independent structure, full-time staff, and dedicated facilities, as highlighted by the participants. A separate, well-equipped building to house the Secretariat could serve as a central hub for coordination and resource mobilisation, facilitating the systematic and sustainable implementation of OH initiatives (Mbabu et al., 2014).
THEME 5: GOVERNANCE
While acknowledging the significant progress made in adopting the OH approach to address zoonotic diseases in Bhutan, participants highlighted several structural and operational challenges that hinder its effectiveness. First, a lack of awareness among policy makers results in inconsistent government support for OH initiatives. Second, despite its “clear structure and working modality,” the BOHSP became outdated in 2023, leaving Bhutan without an up-to-date guiding framework for OH initiatives. Third, the lack of adherence to key commitments, such as the allocation of 1% of the ministerial budgets as outlined in the MOU, has led to heavy reliance on donor funding and ad hoc financial arrangements for implementing OH activities. In addition to these structural issues, the findings revealed inconsistent awareness of the BOHSP among OH stakeholders, particularly at the subnational level. This variation in awareness suggests gaps in dissemination and engagement, which may limit effective implementations and ownership across administrative levels.
To ensure sustainable and effective OH implementation, it is imperative for Bhutan to revise and update the BOHSP, enhance policy makers’ awareness of OH, and establish reliable and sustained funding mechanisms. The BOHSP shows only partial alignment with the OH JPA, which provides a global framework structured around six action tracks: enhancing One Health capacities to strengthen health systems; reducing the risks from emerging and re-emerging zoonotic epidemics and pandemics; controlling and eliminating zoonotic, neglected tropical and vectorborne diseases; strengthening the assessment, management and communication of food safety risks; curbing AMR; and integrating the environment into One Health (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations et al., 2022). Study findings such as weak subnational coordination, workforce gaps, and limited environmental engagement reflect the same challenges the OH JPA aims to address. Aligning the revised BOHSP with these action tracks, guided by its theory of change and pathways,
would offer Bhutan a more coherent and strategic roadmap for advancing multisectoral collaboration and strengthening its OH system. For example, Bangladesh conducted a national OH JPA workshop in 2024, supported by the Asia Pacific Quadripartite One Health team, to revise its One Health Strategy Framework (2017–2021) (World Health Organization, 2024b), aligning it with the OH JPA and engaging multisectoral stakeholders to integrate pandemic lessons and harmonise national priorities with the global framework.
The participants acknowledged the utility of jointly developed prevention and control plans but emphasised the need to develop guidelines and standard operating procedures (SOPs) for other priority zoonotic diseases (World Health Organization, 2024c). These guidelines, if developed through a multisectoral approach involving universities, ministries, research institutions, and other stakeholders, would enhance the standardisation of responses and improve preparedness for health events. For example, in Tanzania, collaborative efforts in formulating national rabies control strategies and SOPs significantly improved coordination and advanced progress toward the elimination of dog-mediated rabies (Mpolya et al., 2017).
STRENGTHS AND LIMITATIONS
This study has several strengths which contribute to its value and reliability. First, this study is the first of its kind in Bhutan to comprehensively assess the implementation of the OH approach, with a focus on zoonotic diseases. Therefore, this study provides novel insights into gaps and opportunities for OH initiatives, offering a foundation for future research and policy development in the country. Second, the inclusion of participants from the public health, animal health, environmental, and academic sectors spanning the national, regional and district levels ensured a diverse range of perspectives. Finally, the application of the COREQ checklist ensured rigorous reporting, enhancing the study’s transparency and reliability.
Despite its strengths, this study has several limitations. It focused primarily on government employees, thereby excluding OH stakeholders from nongovernmental organisations, including the Royal Society for the Protection of Nature and the World Wildlife Fund; private sector representatives such as agricultural enterprises and tourism industry operators; and community actors. This limited inclusion of diverse stakeholder groups may have excluded valuable perspectives and constrained the breadth of the viewpoints necessary to comprehensively understand the challenges and opportunities associated with implementing OH initiatives across various sectors and levels. Representation from environmental and wildlife sectors was limited due to their smaller workforce and recent engagement in OH efforts. Future research should aim to include a wider range of stakeholders to gain a more comprehensive understanding of OH implementation. The sample size was relatively small (n = 22), which may limit the representativeness of perspectives. However, participants were purposively selected for their relevant expertise, and thematic saturation was reached. Similarly, the interviews were relatively short in duration (36–58 min), but they yielded focused and content-rich data due to participant’s familiarity with the subject matter and the use of structured guide based on the five core domains of the OH operational framework. Additionally, the use of online interviews, while addressing logistical constraints, may have limited rapport-building and depth of insight compared with in-person interactions.
CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Bhutan has made significant progress in institutionalising the OH approach through the establishment of frameworks, initiatives, and collaborative activities. These achievements highlight the country’s dedication and commitment to advancing the global OH agenda to combat zoonotic diseases effectively. To address these gaps, Bhutan needs to adopt a multifaceted strategy, with the following key recommendations:
1. Revise and update the Bhutan OH Strategic Plan to align more closely with the OH JPA and reflect current priorities. By implementing these recommendations, Bhutan can further strengthen its OH system, ensuring sustainable progress in combating zoonotic diseases while contributing to global health security.

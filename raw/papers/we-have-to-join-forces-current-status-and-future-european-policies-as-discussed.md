Abstract
Ideally, discussions on the One Health policy should bring together actors from several sectors. This was the case at the conference organised by the One Health European Joint Programme (One Health EJP) “Collaborating to face future One Health challenges in Europe”, which took place from the 19 to the 21 June 2023 in Brussels, Belgium. Here we report on the discussions and interactions that occurred among lead European One Health stakeholders from the animal health, public health, wider environmental (including ecosystem health), and social sciences sectors, coming from the public and private sectors, and non-governmental organisations. The participants included policy makers, risk assessors, risk managers, experts from the academia, as well as representatives of national, European, and international organisations. The conference served two purposes. First, it highlighted the achievements of the One Health EJP on prevention, detection, and response to health threats. Second, it served as a forum to discuss current and future European One Health policies, analysing interactions across sectors, and setting the scene for the One Health policy agenda of tomorrow. In this article, we highlight the main messages and viewpoints of a wide range of European stakeholders on One Health and its future needs. Discussions covered topics such as policies of the European Commission, operationalisation of these policies through EU agencies, the broadening of the One Health concept to increasingly integrate sectors other than public health and animal health, and the role of the private sector in One Health initiatives. These dialogues gave insights into future One Health policies, providing information that integrate the publicly available policy documents, and aiming at shaping the European One Health approach in preparation for upcoming challenges.
Keywords: One Health; policy gaps; science-policy interface; One Health agenda setting; decision making; cross-sector; transdisciplinary
Introduction
One Health is an integrated, unifying approach that recognises the health of humans, animals, and the environment being closely linked and mobilises multiple sectors to work together (Adisasmito et al., 2022). The One Health European Joint Programme (One Health EJP, https://onehealthejp.eu/, Brown et al., 2020) was launched in 2018 as a flagship collaboration among public health, animal health, and food safety institutions in Europe. Since its launch, and until its end in September 2023, it worked to improve preparedness to tackle foodborne zoonoses, antimicrobial resistance (AMR), and emerging
threats (for an overview of the achievements of the One Health EJP, please see the One Health EJP Final Report 2023 (Available at: https://onehealthejp.eu/outcomes/key-documents/ohejp-finalreport-2023, accessed 13 February 2024), the Annex 1 of the Strategic Research and Innovation Agenda (One Health EJP, 2023), the Project Impact Brochures (Available at: https://onehealthejp.eu/ projects/project-impact-brochures, accessed 13 February 2024), and the articles by Taylor et al. (n.d.) and Imberechts et al. (2023). The conference “Collaborating to face future One Health challenges in Europe” (Sepe et al., 2023) thus gave a voice to the wide range of stakeholders involved in One Health, to discuss current and future One Health policy with a focus on Europe. The conference was held from the 19 to the 21 June 2023 in Brussels, Belgium.
The aim of this article is to report on the discussions held during the conference that touched upon the current status of One Health in Europe and shed light on the direction that One Health is taking and could focus on in the future.
One Health impact statement
The One Health EJP conference “Collaborating to face future One Health challenges in Europe” attracted a broad range of participants from different professional backgrounds to facilitate transdisciplinary dialogue on a variety of One Health topics, at the science-policy interface. Reporting discussions among decision makers from across sectors helps improved understanding of policies by providing important insights on: the background and policy context, the point of views of the actors involved, the social milieu, and even the possible compromises behind certain decisions. This is increasingly true for European One Health initiatives, as the EU aims at being a beacon of good One Health practices by bringing together many different sectors, as well as supporting private-public cooperation, under the institutional EU umbrella. The impact of this event is expected to expand upon existing One Health collaborations across sectors and societies to benefit future One Health initiatives.
The One Health EJP: An example of impact at the scientific and policy levels
The first part of the conference was dedicated to discussing and disseminating One Health EJP outcomes (Available at: https:// onehealthejp.eu/outcomes/key-documents/ohejp-final-report-2023; https://onehealthejp.eu/projects/project-impact-brochures, accessed 13 February 2024) (One Health EJP, 2023; Imberechts et al., 2023). Co-funded under Horizon 2020 and with 44 partners across Europe, the One Health EJP (Brown et al., 2020) aligned its activities along the prevent-detect-response axis (Fig. 1), addressing successive steps in setting up One Health preparedness.
The One Health EJP approach to impact was ensured by a defined strategy of dissemination of outcomes and interaction with its partners and stakeholders aimed at supporting the exploitation of results (Taylor et al., n.d.). Ambitious common activities included, for example, a multi-country simulation exercise, aimed at testing practical One Health collaboration across public health, animal health and food safety sectors in an outbreak scenario (Alves et al., 2023).
At the national level, the uptake of One Health EJP outcomes like harmonised approaches, novel tools, and new knowledge, was facilitated by One Health EJP parnter institutes having reference laboratory functions, as well as a close exchange with representatives of line ministries and national authorities. In parallel, interactions with EU agencies and global organisations addressed the European and international needs. National, EU and global stakeholders were represented in specific committees, acting towards operationalisation of the developed One Health solutions and making use of the outcomes to support decision making.
European policies acknowledge the One Health approach as an overarching, cross-cutting approach
The four Directorate Generals of the European Commission (EC) that were represented in a dedicated panel discussion at the conference, Agriculture and Rural Development (DG-AGRI), Environment (DG-ENV), Health and Food Safety (DG-SANTE), and Research and Innovation (DG-RTD), emphasised that the policies laid down by the EC support the operationalisation of One Health, providing a basis to enable cross-sectoral collaboration. This has been reflected, for example, in the setting up of a new Directorate for One Health within DG-SANTE to address One Health issues, particularly AMR (Available at: https://ec.europa.eu/ newsroom/sante/items/758726/en, accessed 13 February 2024).
Operationalisation of the integrated One Health approach is crucial to have long-term impact on health challenges, in Europe as well as globally. One Health is already included in many policies across DGs (Coli and Schebesta, 2023) – importantly in the European Health Union. Efforts should be placed on transdisciplinary and interdisciplinary communication, to integrate knowledge among different scientific disciplines, as well as between scientific disciplines and non-academic stakeholder communities (OECD, 2020), something that the EC looks forward to supporting with thematic approaches. Here, the One Health EJP Glossary (Buschhardt et al., 2021) can prove useful, ensuring a common terminology is available. The One Health approach is seen as a way of working that should not require extra effort, nor should it become an extra silo. In the future, One Health is expected to be implemented as a horizontal approach cutting through various EU policies, to support cross-sectoral collaboration and coordination.
AMR remains on the priority list of the EU health policy (e.g., the “Council Recommendation on stepping up EU actions to combat antimicrobial resistance in a One Health approach” (EU, 2023)), and a prime example of where the One Health approach is needed. The One Health approach is also applied to tackle other One Health issues like foodborne zoonoses and preparedness for emerging threats and has also the potential to address issues traditionally in the domain of other sectors, such as air pollution, climate change, and biodiversity loss.

The EC representatives agreed to view European One Health as an approach capable of addressing not only prevention, detection, and response to health threats but also health improvement and promotion, and recovery from health crises.
The new One Health cross-agency task force: A cross-sectoral EUstructure collaboration operationalising European One Health
On the operational side, the conference saw a dialogue among representatives of EU agencies involved in One Health: the European Environment Agency (EEA), the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC), the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), and the European Medicines Agency (EMA). The major update was the launch of the One Health crossagency task force, a collaboration at the agency level between the aforementioned agencies and the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) (Bronzwaer et al., 2022; ECDC et al., 2023). The One Health cross-agency task force builds on long-standing collaborations among the agencies. The task force will ensure strategic coordination and strengthen One Health capacities within and across the agencies.
One important point was that the joint approach at the agencies’ level could encourage their networks and stakeholders to pursue cross-sectoral collaborations, at both national and European levels. The additional added value of having an institutionalised task force will allow the agencies to “speak with one voice”, easing engagement with the stakeholders and improving advocacy efficacy by emphasising shared positions.
Challenges were also discussed. The task force is a bottom-up initiative, promoted by the agencies themselves; as yet there is no clear governance. The need for a legislative framework to maximise the benefit of such an alliance is clear (FAO, 2020a). Already, taking full advantage of their complementary mandates, the agencies can start to examine each other’s activities to align data, methodologies, perceptions, and interpretation, aiming, eventually, “to align scientific advice itself with the One Health approach” (ECDC et al., 2023).
The environmental component of One Health is nominally included in European One Health initiatives, but integration is under way
The wider environmental sector (Adisasmito et al., 2022) has always been a component of the classical One Health humananimal-environment triad (Cumming and Cumming, 2015; Schelling and Zinsstag, 2015). However, until recently, it was often overlooked, with the focus being placed on human and animal health (Essack, 2018; Humboldt-Dachroeden et al., 2020). While EU policies acknowledge the importance of integrating the environmental sector to address One Health issues (see, e.g. the Green Deal policy frameworks Farm to Fork Strategy (EC, 2020a), and Biodiversity Strategy (EU, 2020)), operational initiatives have been lagging behind. In this light, the contribution of EEA with its knowledge and resources to the One Health cross-agency task force brings a particularly appreciated added value.
Involvement of the environmental sector is already evident in some new initiatives, for example, those with a focus on wastewater surveillance. The environmental sector has the potential of contributing to actions promoting human health and animal health besides surveillance and risk assessment; for example, it should be considered when looking at food systems and consumption, to reconcile sustainable production with safe and healthy diets.
The wider environment, including ecosystems (Adisasmito et al., 2022), should be seen as part of a complex social-ecological system,
being influenced, influencing, and ultimately entangled with the behaviours and well-being of all organisms, including humans, animals and plants. For example, pollution and chemicals in the environment are often overlooked by traditional One Health, despite they influence typical One Health issues like AMR and emerging diseases.
To expand the benefits of One Health solutions we should consider a better integration of social sciences
One Health has been dominated by life scientists, however, interaction and engagement with target audiences (e.g., consumers, civil society, producers, medical doctors, and veterinarians), something that does not necessarily fall within their expertise, has long been acknowledged as important (Schelling and Zinsstag, 2015; Whittaker, 2021). Without bringing on board the target audiences, One Health solutions fall short of reaching impact. As corollary, the panellists and the audience of the conference were in general agreement that we should pave the way for the integration of social sciences in One Health initiatives.
The advantages are immediately clear. Social sciences can help us understand society’s behaviours, concerns, skills, knowledge, daily life and approaches to challenges, to co-design and set up engagement activities that are behaviourally informed. One example where social sciences are crucial to reach impact is creating “enabling food environments” (EU Food Policy Coalition, 2021). Furthermore, the integration of social sciences in One Health will benefit policy making, because policies should take into consideration behaviours of their target to be effective.
The speakers acknowledged that the increase in complexity resulting from the integration of additional sectors and fields in One Health initiatives will also result in more burdensome project management, as well as a risk of losing focus. This, however, will likely be compensated by the added value. Transparency, openness (also when it comes to communicating uncertainties) and evidence-based were highlighted as essential for interacting with the target audiences – including the media.
Expanding the role of the private sector
One Health needs to synthesise and integrate aspects from different sectors to improve our common understanding and actions, because ultimately One Health issues can impact the whole society, including the economy. In this context, increased interaction with the private sector is also to be expected, and this was discussed at the conference. For example, it was discussed that European public initiatives could team up with the private sector to address particular areas of market failures, such as development of new antibiotics. A unifying approach could tackle AMR from the prevention side (responsible use), research and development, and production side (responsible manufacturing), engaging professional organisations of medical doctors and veterinarians as well as pharmaceutical industries.
Private companies are themselves looking with increased interest at sustainable innovations and One Health, recognising the added value of working together and with the public sector. From their viewpoint, there appears to be a willingness to be involved early on in One Health activities, and to build on solutions developed in One Health projects. How the private sector will in practice become integrated in future European activities remains to be seen.

The European One Health strategy should be global, because, as often remarked during the conference, One Health issues are global issues. It has to be inclusive, with a focus on equity and fair representation, without which trust and collaboration are impossible. It must keep refining the integration of the human health, animal health, and wider environmental sectors, as well as social sciences, so that all the gears are well-oiled. This includes the understanding and integration of each other’s points of view, priorities, languages, methods, data, risk assessment, and risk management.
Indicators to monitor and evaluate One Healthness are needed. These should not be only limited to cost-benefit analyses but also include other factors. For example, mechanisms to monitor to what extent One Health solutions developed in research projects find their way into practical applications, and how easy it is for the end users to find, apply, and further develop them.
Another aspect often mentioned to be crucial for preparing for the future was education, from programmes in schools to training for the private sector. The experience from One Health EJP is that education – and learning – are life-long, and of benefit across lifeand career-stages.
Major tools with which Europe will address One Health implementation and research needs are the EU4Health (EU, 2021a) and the Horizon Europe programmes (EU, 2021b). Indeed, a number of new Partnerships funded under Horizon Europe that focus on preparedness, public health, global health, and animal health
and welfare include the One Health approach, however, an allencompassing One Health initiative is lacking.
The conference was closed with high expectations. The future European One Health approach “will have to be inclusive, cohesive and bold”, with elements of “trust, openness and endurance”. Its aim will be “broad”, addressing the whole detect-prevent-respond axis, and also including recovery from crises and promotion of health. “The ambition level will be set high”, and “Europe will provide global leadership”.
We are not there yet, but we are on the right path – indeed the only path to face the One Health challenges of tomorrow. As Kerstin Rosenow (DG-AGRI) stressed “We have to join forces, we have no choice”.

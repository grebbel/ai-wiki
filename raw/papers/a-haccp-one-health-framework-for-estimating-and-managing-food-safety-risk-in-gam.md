Abstract
Background: Game meat is consumed in Zambia, yet the supply chain remains weakly regulated compared with that of domestic livestock systems. Wild game, harvested under uncontrolled conditions, often lack veterinary oversight, biosecurity, or hygienic infrastructure, creating food safety risk. In contrast, farmed game benefits from some biosecurity practices, although gaps remain. Understanding these risks is essential for safeguarding public health and promoting sustainable wildlife management. Aim: This study aimed to identify hazardous practices and potential food safety hazards in Zambia’s farmed and wild game meat supply chains and to explore how a One Health (OH) approach, which is an integrative framework that links human, animal, and environmental health, can be applied to manage these risks. Methods: A qualitative mixed-methods design was applied in the Lusaka district, combining 18 key informant interviews, 4 direct observations from local butcheries, and hazard analysis using the Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point (HACCP) framework. The participants included hunters, farmers, butchers, regulators, researchers, conservationists, and consumers. The data were thematically coded using grounded theory, and hazards were then identified from the data using the HACCP framework, with risks ranked by severity and likelihood across sourcing, processing, distribution, sales, consumption and by-products. Findings: Wild game meat poses the greatest food safety risk due to the absence of ante-mortem inspections, unhygienic slaughter and handling in the field, gastrointestinal tract damage during shooting, incomplete bleeding, and transport in open vehicles. Cultural practices such as consuming spoiled meat further increase food safety hazards. Farmed game meat demonstrated lower risks, supported by biosecurity measures, veterinary inspections, and cold storage. However, hazards persist, particularly from broken cold chains and delayed evisceration. Conclusions: Wild game meat consistently presents greater food safety hazards than farmed game meat does, although both require improved oversight. This article shows how an OH approach that integrates veterinary, public health, and environmental (processing and handling) could support the management of food safety risks. The recommended measures include standardized inspections, stronger biosecurity measures, mobile hygienic processing units, cold chain enforcement, consumer education, and regulatory reform. Interdisciplinary collaboration is critical for safer game meat supply chains in Zambia.
One Health impact statement
This article demonstrates how an OH approach can effectively manage food safety risks in Zambia’s game meat supply chain, benefiting public health, animal welfare, and the environment. By fostering collaboration among the human health, veterinary, environmental, and regulatory sectors, this study identified unsafe food handling and processing practices, associated hazards, and developed targeted interventions that are not possible through isolated efforts. The integration of academic research with practical knowledge from local butchers, game farmers, and government officials enabled the co-production of actionable, context-specific solutions such as improved traceability and biosecurity practices that enhance food safety and reduce disease transmission from game animals to humans through meat handling and consumption. This transdisciplinary strategy has a greater impact by safeguarding both urban consumers and rural livelihoods, supporting sustainable wildlife management, and informing policies for safer, more resilient food systems.

Introduction
Game meat is used to describe meat from game animals or wild animals that have been hunted legally (Lindsey et al., 2015). In some instances, it is called wild game meat (Gomes-Neves et al., 2021; Fantechi et al., 2022). It is not to be confused with bush meat, which is meat from wild animals that has been acquired by means of illegal hunting, without licences/permits; being practiced in areas where hunting is prohibited; the use of prohibited methods; and the killing of protected species, sexes, or ages of animals (Lindsey et al., 2015). Game meat is further categorized as farmed or wild game depending on its source. The European Union Regulation (EC) No. 853/2004 defines wild game “as wild ungulates and lagomorphs, as well as other land mammals that are hunted for human consumption and are considered to be wild game under the applicable law in the Member State concerned, including mammals living in enclosed territory under conditions of freedom similar to those of wild game” whilst farmed game refers to “farmed ratites and farmed land mammals other than domestic ungulates (Bubalus and Bison species), porcine, ovine and caprine animals, and domestic solipeds”.
The production of game meat has been increasing, with an estimated global production of approximately 2 million tons in 2016 (Rawal et al., 2019). The United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE) saw an increased production from US$190 million in 2001 to US$365 million in 2011 (UNECE and FAO, 2018). In 2013, UNECE produced 400,000 tons of game meat valued at approximately $850 million (Rawal et al., 2019). To ensure its safety in Europe, the main regulatory framework that regulates game meat is Regulation (EC) No 853/2004. Many countries in Europe have adopted this regulatory framework through national food security agencies, such as Italy (Ramanzin et al., 2010), Portugal (Abrantes et al., 2023), and Germany (Atanassova et al., 2008), for the purpose of regulating the domestic and international game meat trade. Some countries have detailed guidelines to guarantee appropriate methods for slaughtering and dressing carcasses (Ramanzin et al., 2010), such as the United Kingdom (United Kingdom Wild Game Guidance of 2022) and other standards; for example, Australia has the Australian Standard for the Hygienic Production of Wild Game Meat for Human Consumption (AS 4464: 2007).
In Africa, very few countries have formalized game meat systems (game meat system that is regulated) (Mukanganwa et al., 2025). Examples are South Africa (Bekker et al., 2011), Namibia (Lindsey, 2011), Botswana (Barnes, 2001), Zambia (FAO et al., 2024), and Tanzania, still in its infancy (Kadigi et al., 2023). South Africa is the largest exporter in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) region, exporting approximately 3010 tons of game meat per year (Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment, South Africa, 2023). Namibia is another major SADC game meat producer, with an annual output of approximately 17,637–28,660 tons. To ensure its safety, South Africa has Standards for PostMortem Meat Inspection and Hygiene Control at Game Meat Establishments (VPN/10/2007-01); Standard for the microbiological monitoring of meat, process hygiene and cleaning (VPN/15/201001); and Standard for ante- and post-mortem meat inspection and hygiene control at the point of game harvest (VPN/09/2017-01). These veterinary procedure notices (VPNs) were developed for the European Union market and do not apply to the local market (Van Der Merwe et al., 2013). For domestic game meat, the country is working on the South African Game Meat Regulations, which are in the draft phase. Botswana has the Botswana S.I. 56, of 2007, which covers both domestic and game meat. Namibia has Guidelines for the Harvesting and Processing of Wild Game in Namibia of 2016.
In Zambia, approximately 700 tons of game meat are produced each year. An estimated 330 tons come from resident hunters (which constitute wild game meat), and 390 come from game ranches (which constitute farmed game meat) (FAO et al., 2024).
Resident hunters hunt in game management areas (GMAs), which are considered buffer zones immediately surrounding national parks, where human settlement, limited agricultural activity, and legal game hunting are allowed (Phiri et al., 2011). Farmed game meat comes from fenced game ranches (FAO et al., 2024) where they are protected from predation, and more importantly, disease control measures can be applied, whereas free-ranging or wild game is prone to predation, and disease control measures are difficult to institute (Magwedere et al., 2015). In addition, farmed game are maintained in more controlled environments with limited contact with other wild or domestic species, thereby reducing opportunities for cross-species transmission of pathogens (Ferran et al., 2021). In addition, game meat farming can also lead to improved biodiversity (Bersaglio et al., 2025). Farmed game is intentionally reared to produce meat and hides (Whyte et al., 2011). Comparing the farmed environment with the wild environment is worthwhile since these two contexts are very different, but both environments require control measures to minimize biological hazards and contamination risks in game meat. In terms of the game meat safety regulation framework, there are no specific game meat safety regulations or guidelines. The domestic livestock meat regulation framework is borrowed from the domestic meat supply chain and used for the regulation and control of game meat. Only the sourcing (hunting) stage has specific game meat regulations, which are conservation regulations; these are the regulations that formalize the game meat trade. The following ministries (some with overlapping mandates) are involved in the game meat supply chain: sourcing, the Ministry of Tourism and Natural Resources (which is responsible for wildlife), the Ministry of Livestock and Fisheries (animal welfare and health), which is responsible for sourcing and processing. The Ministry of Health (food safety) is responsible for processing, transportation, selling, and consumption (health promotion), and the Ministry of Local Government (food safety), which is responsible for butchery inspections (Mukanganwa et al., 2025).
Ranucci et al. (2021) highlighted that game meat production strongly differs from domestic meat production, as peculiar factors present in the field and in the steps prior to transfer to a gamehandling establishment can influence the hygiene of the carcasses and, therefore, of the game meat. This puts the entire game meat chain at risk from hunting to consumption (Paige et al., 2014; D’Cruze et al., 2020). This means that borrowing the domestic livestock meat regulation framework is not enough, as production is different. With respect to safety, Gill (2007) noted that the safety of game meat is closely associated with the hunting process and hygiene/temperature in the subsequent handling and transport of carcasses. In addition, Needham et al. (2023) stated that food safety is one of the greatest challenges for the game meat supply chain. Identifying high-risk practices and improving sanitary and animal welfare conditions along supply chains are fundamental to reducing food safety risk (Kock and Caceres-Escobar, 2022). To protect the public from game meat-associated risks, van Vliet et al. (2022) underscored the importance of understanding the practices that shape food safety along the supply chain for better management of vectors of pathogens. It is therefore important to identify hazardous practices and potential hazards along the game meat supply chain through a system-based approach for example Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point (HACCP) and propose how the hazardous practices and potential hazards can be managed using an OH approach that links human, animal, and environmental health to guide the management of the game meat supply chain through targeted monitoring, surveillance and risk prevention strategies.
HACCP is a tool for assessing hazards and establishing control systems that focus on control measures for significant hazards along the food chain rather than relying mainly on end-product testing (FAO/WHO, 2009). This approach has been suggested for the management of hazards in the game meat chain. For example, the Code of Hygienic Practice for Meat (CAC/RCP

The OH approach has since been suggested as an approach to manage food safety risks (Aggarwal and Ramachandran, 2020). Nguyen-Viet et al. This interdisciplinary team makes risk mitigation more effective (Lâm et al., 2024). As already established, there is an interconnectedness and overlapping of mandates along Zambia’s game meat supply chain. If food safety risks are to be managed, involved ministries and departments need to go beyond disciplinary boundaries and embrace intersectoral collaboration through the OH lens. Hence, this study aimed to identify hazardous practices, potential hazards, and qualitatively estimate food safety risk along Zambia’s farmed and wild game meat supply chain; and propose how intersectoral collaboration through the OH approach can manage these risks.
Methods
STUDY DESIGN
Qualitative mixed methods comprising three components key informant interviews, butchery observations, hazard identification and qualitative risk assessment using a HACCP framework were employed for data collection (Fig. 1). The primary data were collected from October 2023 to February 2024.
Key informant interviews
The first key informants were identified in a stakeholder mapping process using the purposive sampling (Palinkas et al., 2015) followed by snowballing sampling (Heckathorn, 2011). The inclusion criterion was individuals with professional or operational knowledge of the game meat trade; the exclusion criterion included a lack of relevant expertise or refusal to provide informed consent. Snowballing sampling was repeated until the data saturation point was reached. In-depth interviews were conducted with 18 key informants. The interview guide is provided in the supplementary material. The interviewed individuals were selected from game farmers/ranchers, butchers, national focal officers, consultants, researchers, academicians (University of Zambia, School of Veterinary Medicine), senior members of organizations that responsible for the governance of game and game meat in Zambia and conservationist (Table 1). Conservationist were interviewed as there are a number of conservation organization working with local people to promote legal game meat as way to stop bush meat trade and to sustain wildlife management. All of the interviewees were also game meat consumers. The interviews were conducted by the authors. All the interviews were conducted by the authors.
Observations
Structured, non-participant, and direct observations were conducted, focusing on hygiene and sanitation practices, the presence of a cold chain, and the sale of game versus livestock meat. The observations were performed at four local butcheries. A notepad was used to record the observations. Qualitative data was collected. At the end of each observation day, the observations were entered into a Microsoft Word document. The authors conducted all the observations.
Hazard identification and qualitative risk assessment
Food safety hazards associated with game meat were assessed using the HACCP framework as recommended by the Codex Alimentarius Commission (FAO and WHO 2023). Information from key informant interviews and observations was systematically analysed to identify hazardous practices, potential hazards and perform a risk assessment along the game meat supply chain (sourcing, processing, distribution, sales, consumption, and by-products).

STUDY AREA
The study was conducted in Lusaka district, Zambia. Lusaka is the district where a high volume of legal game meat is traded and consumed. All the participants interviewed were based in this district and were directly involved in the game meat supply chain. It has an estimated population of approximately 3 million as per the 2022 census according to the Zambia Statistics Agency (2023). It is located at −15.41° latitude and 28.29° longitude and is situated at an elevation of 1277 m above sea level (as obtained from Google Earth).
DATA COLLECTION, TRANSLATION AND TRANSCRIPTION
The interviews were conducted in either English, Nyanja, or a combination of both. In cases where the informant was not fluent in English, a Nyanja field assistant acted as an interpreter. The interview guides were drafted by the first author, guided partially by the WHO interim document (WHO et al., 2021). The interviews were structured in three sections: general thoughts on the game meat trade in Zambia, understanding the game meat supply chain, and other organizations or individuals who had game meat knowledge. The interviews ranged from 30 min to 1 h 40 min and took place in offices, over phone calls, Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and Google Meet. In-person interviews were recorded using a recording device. Phone interviews were recorded on the phone. Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and Google Meet interviews were recorded on a computer. In all these interviews, consent was sought first. The in-person and phone-recorded interviews were transferred to a computer for transcription. Interviews that were conducted in Nyanja were translated into English by writing down what was said in the audio in a Microsoft Word file. English interviews were transferred to a computer for transcription. Artificial intelligence (AI) Whisper, which is a function that is embedded in Python software, was used for data transcription. The recorded audios were input into the application, and Microsoft Word text transcripts were output. The interviews were transcribed in English. The translated Nyanja Microsoft Word transcripts and transcribed English Microsoft Word
transcripts were then uploaded to NVivo 12 for coding (qualitative data analysis).
THEMATIC CONTENT ANALYSIS
Grounded theory was used for coding as previously done by Milstein et al. (2020) and Goodall (2022). This approach is used when little is known about the phenomena being studied (Chun Tie et al., 2019). As the hallmark of grounded theory, concurrent data generation and analysis using the constant comparative analysis iterative process was done (Birks et al., 2019). The data were coded and guided by the following supply chain stages/ themes: species, sourcing (farmed and wild), processing (farmed and wild), distribution (farmed and wild), selling, consumption and by-products. The analysis was performed through initial coding, which fractured the data, and then theoretical coding, which weaved the fractured data back together again into an organized theory (Chun Tie et al., 2019). Advanced coding was then used to facilitate the integration of the final grounded theory (Birks et al., 2019; Chun Tie et al., 2019).
ETHICAL CLEARANCE AND PARTICIPANT CONSENT
Ethical clearance was obtained from the Tropical Diseases Research Centre (“Wildlife meat trade in Zambia and bacterial zoonoses” TDREC/124/09/23). Research permits were obtained from government agencies and departments that required a permit to carry out interviews. The consent letter was given to the informants before the interviews, and consent was obtained before the interview process commenced. For the online interviews, the authors read the consent before the interviews.
Results
DESCRIPTION OF THE WILD AND FARMED GAME MEAT SUPPLY CHAIN
Sourcing Species
The study revealed that buffalo (Syncerus caffer), kudu (Tragelaphus strepsiceros), impala (Aepyceros melampus), warthog (Phacochoerus africanus), hippopotamus (Hippopotamus amphibious), and the lechwe antelopes (Kobus sp.) are the most consumed species. One informant reported the following:
“Some game species are more preferred by consumers. The reason is because of their availability, for instance, the buffalo, the lechwe antelopes, warthog, hippo, they have unique taste characteristics”.
The estimated production of farmed and wild game meat reported was approximately 700 tons per year.

When game hunters enter the forest, they identify the animal, after which the animal is shot. There are instances in where animals are shot and they do not die immediately. Farmed game meat
The informants revealed the presence of several biosecurity measures, such as the fencing of farms and the monitoring of disease, as reported by one of the informants:
“But what normally happens at game farms is that the owners of the animals have disease control measures, which they do frequently depending on their management system. There is certainty that the animals are being kept almost the same way as livestock.”
The informants mentioned that at the ranch, it is different from the abattoir for livestock. However, personal hygiene is practiced. They have the prerequisites that are needed to establish an abattoir. These are facilities such as toilets and changing rooms. Ante-mortem inspections are performed by means of health assessment of the game ranch rather than individual animals, since it is difficult to bring the animals to a central place for inspection before shooting. If the ranch passes, a certificate is given, which is valid for a year depending on the findings. This certification process acts as an anti-mortem certificate. Allowing the culling of the animals.
Hunting was said to occur all year, that is, during the rainy and dry seasons. As reported by this key informant:
“Farmed game meat, we get it throughout the year since it comes from game ranches. Wild game meat, we get it when the hunting season is open usually from September to December from resident hunters. Even when the season is closed, we still get some from people who hunt in GMA off hunting season.”
The farmed game meat sourcing starts with the client who reaches out to the rancher and lets the rancher know the animal(s) of interest. The owner of the game range has private scouts who scout for the animals, while a vehicle is used for scouting. When the animal has been found, it is shot, and they collect it into the vehicle, depending on how far they have gone. The hunters are trained shooters; however, they are not trained in game meat handling. As much as the shooters are trained, instances of damage to the gastrointestinal tract during shooting were reported.
The vehicle drives from the forest to the game establishment area. The processing area can be anywhere within the vicinity of the establishment; if the farmhouse is far away, there are commercialized places in the game establishment. The vehicle will
drive to that central place, and the animal will be moved from the vehicle.
There are circumstances in which the client wants more than one animal. In this case, the animal is not carried to the game establishment area for processing; instead, just after shooting, the cavity of the carcass is cut open, and the internal organs are removed in the field. The carcass is then put in the truck, and the hunters continue to hunt more animals. In this case, by the time the carcass reaches the central place, the intestines have already been removed. One informant illustrated this by using an impala example:
“So, there are times they want more than one impala. In these times, just after they shot the first impala, they bleed it, remove the viscera, and put it in a central place, then go ahead with the hunt until they have reached the number of impalas they need.”
Processing Wild meat
After shooting, the slaughtering process starts in the forest, and it is performed by the hunters themselves. Very few of these hunters have the expertise needed to perform the slaughtering process properly, for example, to allow complete bleeding. The informants mentioned that, owing to improper training in some instances, bleeding is not performed properly. The slaughtering process is performed on the ground; however, in some cases, the hunters hang the animal in a tree to assist with the slaughtering process. In some circumstances, the hunters spread leaves and do the slaughtering on top of the leaves, or they spread the skins of already slaughtered animal(s) and perform the slaughtering process on top of the skins (which can be of the same species or of a different species). After the slaughtering process, the meat can leave the GMA either fresh or dried. If the meat is to be dried, the meat is dried using fire, or commercial dryers are sometimes used. Depending on the season, if it is not smoked and the hunters do not have refrigerators, it might start to spoil because of exposure to environmental microbes. One of the informants mentioned that, strangely, some people who eat game meat, especially dry game meat, prefer it when it has an ‘off smell’, such as a putrefaction and a rotting smell. The reason is that it makes them feel like they are actually eating game meat; hence, sometimes it is left to rot (spoil) intentionally. Six informants revealed that the handling process is not sanitary, considering that it happens in the forest, where there is no water or proper infrastructure.
The processing was described as usually very unhygienic. One informant reported that:
“The process is very basic, very rudimentary, and unhygienic most of the time, with a lot of exposure to a lot of environmental and gastrointestinal bacteria.”
It was revealed that around 1976, following high tuberculosis cases at national parks such as the Lochinvar National Park, the government requested that the Kafue lechwe meat be screened through proper meat inspection. Hence, the government built an abattoir, which only functioned for about 4 years. One informant highlighted the following:
“They built abattoirs, which just worked for some time afterwards. When you go there, you will find the slab and the rail dressing systems. The infrastructure has actually crumbled. For instance, there is an abattoir in South Luangwa; the facilities are there, but they are rarely used. There are also slaughter facilities, both in Lochinvar and in South Luangwa, but they are also rarely used.”
The other place that was reported to have an abattoir is the Bangweulu wetlands. It was said that the government gives out permits to an institution or individuals who have to slaughter some animals in this area for sale to the communities using this abattoir.

They remove the skin when they reach the game establishment area. The carcasses are then cleaned with pressurized water. If pressurized water is not available, a cloth is used to clean the carcass and remove the blood. The hooves and head are then removed. It is at that stage that a veterinarian is contacted to go to the ranch to conduct a post-mortem meat inspection. With respect to abattoirs, some of the ranches were said to have abattoirs, and all slaughter facilities were registered under the Animal Health Act of 2010. It was also highlighted that requirements for an abattoir for livestock differ from those for wildlife. Therefore, they generally have a small game establishment area where they process the animals. The farmed game process was described as very organized and sanitary. In terms of sanitation, nine informants said it was sanitary. One informant said it was not sanitary, whereas the other eight did not respond to the question. Protective clothing in the form of overalls and gum boots was reported to be used only when the carcass reaches the game establishment area. No use of protective clothing (masks, disposable gloves, glasses) was reported. It was revealed that no hygiene standards were followed.
Distribution
Wild game meat
In terms of distribution, the majority of the informants mentioned that most of the time, they use open vans, and sometimes, they use refrigerated trucks. The use of cooler boxes was also highlighted in some circumstances. The Veterinary Department stated that regulations that are yet to be assented to hunters should make effort to reduce the level of pathogens by transporting the meat in vessels that are not going to contaminate the product.
Farmed game meat
The study revealed that during transportation, the cold chain is sometimes maintained and sometimes broken. Six informants mentioned that they use refrigerated trucks. Five said that they occasionally use refrigerated trucks. The remaining did not respond to the question. One informant reported that:
“I have not seen a rancher with a refrigerated truck, but at times, when there is an outlet that wants a lot of game meat. The clients send the refrigerated truck to the ranch. After the meat has been inspected, it is put in a refrigerated truck. Other ranchers, what they are doing is they are also supplying to targeted individual clients; they cut the carcass, freeze it, put it in a cooler box, and deliver it.”
It was also highlighted that the hygienic conditions are sometimes poor. The use of open vans was also pointed out. The butchers reported that because there are not a lot of suppliers of game meat, the suppliers do not put much effort in quality delivery systems knowing that they do not have competition.
Selling (observations)
It was observed that all the butcheries were selling both game meat (different species) and domestic meat in the same butchery. The meat was properly stored and displayed, and a cold chain was being observed. Three butcheries were sanitary; one was not. The sanitary assessment was based on observable hygiene indicators, including the cleanliness of refrigerators, cutting surfaces, and equipment, use of protective clothing by staff, absence of flies or visible contamination, proper waste disposal, and general maintenance of the premises. The non-sanitary butchery showed unclean refrigerators, poor surface hygiene, lack of protective clothing, and evidence of fly infestation.
Consumption
All the informants reported that the most common method of preparation is boiling and braising. It was reported that the Department of Veterinary Services is working to discourage consumption of game meat from GMAs as a result of unsafe handling and contamination risks that have been reported.
By-products
The practice of leaving the by-products at the place of slaughtering in the forest during game processing for the scavengers (hyenas and vultures) to eat was reported. It was reported that for some species, by-products are given to workers, whereas for others, they take them home to feed their dogs. The leaving of waste to decompose in the field was also mentioned by the informants.

Discussion
The most frequently traded group reported in this study was the ungulates. Woolhouse and Gowtage-Sequeria (2005) classified ungulates as an important zoonotic host group because they serve as intermediate hosts for many pathogens and are commonly hunted and consumed. However, empirical evidence from confirmed human infections shows that bats and primates account for most zoonotic spillover events, followed by wild rodents and, to a lesser extent, buffalo and wild pigs (Cupertino et al., 2020). This pattern reflects differences in transmission routes and host–pathogen ecology. Spillover from bats and primates often occurs through direct contact with blood, secretions, or bites during hunting and butchering, or via ingestion of undercooked meat, facilitating viral entry into humans (Bonwitt et al., 2018; Ellwanger and Chies, 2021). In contrast, zoonotic transmission from ungulates is typically associated with bacterial or parasitic pathogens transmitted through contaminated meat, aerosols, or cuts during carcass handling, rather than through viral shedding (Godfroid, 2002; Plowright et al., 2017). All this indicates that while ungulates are epidemiologically significant due to frequent human–animal interactions, their role in direct spillover may be lower than taxa with higher viral diversity and more efficient directcontact transmission routes. However, it is important to note that without human case data and confirmatory diagnostics on zoonotic and emerging infectious disease pathogens transmitted or derived from wildlife species, it is not possible to determine with certainty the importance or risk of these hosts, reservoirs, or genetic origins (Kock and Caceres-Escobar, 2022). This study revealed significant differences in food safety risk between wild and farmed game meat across the sourcing, processing, and distribution stages.
Wild game meat poses the greatest risk, primarily because of the absence of ante-mortem inspection, unhygienic field processing, and inadequate training of hunters in carcass handling. Wild game meat sourced from GMAs was characterized by the absence of ante-mortem inspection, unhygienic handling, and poor slaughtering practices, which greatly increase the risk of zoonotic disease transmission. These findings corroborate van Schalkwyk and Hoffman (2016), who underscored the role of ante-mortem inspections in mitigating zoonotic spillover. Unhygienic field processing contributes to high contamination risk, as previously reported by Mirceta et al. (2017). In their study, wild boars from hunting grounds that were eviscerated in the field presented higher microbiological aerobic colony counts and Enterobacteriaceae counts than carcasses eviscerated within game handling facilities did. Furthermore, processing in the field without potable water agrees with observations by Mirceta et al. (2017), who reported higher microbial counts in carcasses eviscerated under field conditions than in those processed in handling facilities. The lack of protective clothing (masks, disposable gloves, glasses) that was reported by key informants mirrors what was reported in a study by Abrantes et al. (2023); in their study, the majority indicated not using masks and protective glasses during game meat processing. The importance of wearing gloves as a measure for protection against possible infection was highlighted by Korkmaz et al. (2022). Game species are vehicles and reservoirs of some zoonotic pathogens (Akpan et al., 2025). LeBreton et al. (2006) reported that there is a lack of awareness and precautionary measures taken among people who come into contact with game meat; as a result, the opportunity for new zoonotic pathogens to spill over into humans remains high.
In contrast, farmed game meat demonstrated relatively better hygiene and biosecurity, with practices such as farm-level certification, veterinary post-mortem inspections, and, in some cases, access to cold storage facilities. Nevertheless, risks remain, particularly gastrointestinal tract damage during shooting and inconsistent maintenance of the cold chain during transportation. As noted by Sauvala et al. (2019), gut shots significantly increase bacterial contamination. In a study by Membré et al. (2011), the cold chain seemed to have an effect on the hygiene quality of game meat.
Moreover, the absence of standardized evisceration timelines in Zambia contrasts with South African standards and Namibian game guidelines, which allow a maximum of 10 min between shooting evisceration (Bekker et al., 2011; van Schalkwyk and Hoffman, 2016), and Botswana S.I. 56, of 2007, which states that evisceration should be carried out as soon as possible following the shooting, if applicable, no later than 45 minutes. These gaps highlight the need for Zambia to adopt harmonized standards to minimize microbial risk.
Distribution practices have emerged as a medium for farmed to high-risk for wild game meat chains. The use of open vans and the frequent breakdown of the cold chain were highlighted by informants and corresponds with studies that categorize transportation as a critical high-risk step (Giuggioli et al., 2018; Nkosi et al., 2023), mirroring the high-risk transport conditions described by Korkmaz et al. (2022) and Nkosi et al. (2023). Poor vehicle hygiene and the stacking of carcasses exacerbate crosscontamination risks (Korkmaz et al., 2022). Strengthening cold chain infrastructure and enforcing sanitary transport regulations could therefore substantially improve game meat safety.
The management of by-products presents additional hazards. The practice of leaving by-products in the forest or feeding them to dogs, as observed in this study, increases the likelihood of pathogen transmission to scavengers, domestic animals, and ultimately humans. Similarly, Vicente et al. (2011) and Lizana et al. (2022) cautioned against such disposal methods, noting their potential to perpetuate disease cycles.
Consumption patterns also reflect a significant risk factor. While boiling and thorough cooking reduce microbial hazards (Van Vliet et al., 2022). Mpalang et al. (2013) highlighted the importance of understanding cooking practices in mitigating foodborne disease. The cultural preference for partially spoiled meat, identified in this study, may exacerbate public health risks.

Taken together, these findings suggest that wild game meat poses consistently greater health risks than farmed game meat does because of the lack of inspection, hygiene, and infrastructure. Farmed game meat, while comparatively safer, remains vulnerable to contamination risks during shooting, processing, and distribution. The integration of these results with the existing literature underscores the urgent need for regulatory reforms, enhanced training of hunters and meat handlers, and improved infrastructure for meat inspection and cold chain maintenance. Strengthening these aspects is essential to safeguarding public health and ensuring the sustainability of the game meat sector in Zambia. Increasing awareness of the risks and prevention methods associated with wildlife contact and game meat consumption helps hunters take steps toward risk reduction (Hedman et al., 2020) during game and game meat handling. The CAC/RCP 58-2005, which covers farmed and wild game meat, states that personal hygiene should include protective clothing that can be effectively cleaned and minimize the accumulation of contaminants. The Illinois Department of Agriculture recommends avoiding contact with the hide, brain, spinal cord, spleen, eyes, tonsils, or lymph nodes (Hedman et al., 2020).

LIMITATIONS
This study has several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, the assessment relied primarily on qualitative data from key informants and observations within the Lusaka district and did not include quantitative aspects, for instance, the number of animals hunted per year, hunters, and butcheries selling game meat. In addition, the categorization of hazards as “low,” “medium,” or “high” reflects perceived and contextual risk rather than measured contamination levels.
CONCLUSIONS
The potential food safety risk posed by farmed game meat is less than that posed by wild game meat. The factors differentiating the two methods are biosecurity, an element of disease control, post-mortem inspections and the availability of the game meat establishment area. The study proposes implementing the OH approach through transdisciplinary integration. Biosecurity measures can protect workers from direct contact with animals or meat during sourcing, processing and distribution. In addition, it protects ranched animals from wild animals and, by doing so, limits zoonotic spillover. Inspections ensure that the meat that is entering the market is safe for human consumption. The creation of a game establishment area would strengthen hygiene compliance. Community-based training can increase consumer awareness and promote safe consumption. Given the interconnectivity of the global food supply chain, the findings of this study can help with a better understanding of how OH can be integrated into the game meat supply chain and shape future interventions to reduce the associated risks. Future research integrating laboratory testing, larger geographical coverage, and quantitative risk assessment would strengthen the evidence base for national food safety interventions.

# Learning from history of natural disasters in the Sahel: a comprehensive analysis and lessons for future resilience

## TL;DR

Using EM-DAT records from 1960 to 2020, this study shows that Sahel disaster burdens are dominated by floods and biological hazards in frequency, while drought has the deepest population-level consequences.

## Key findings

- The study analyzes 1,000 disaster events across 16 Sahelian countries and separates patterns by western, central, and eastern subregions.
- Hydrological hazards are most frequent, while biological hazards are also prominent across the dataset.
- Drought occurs less often than floods but accounts for a disproportionate share of affected populations.
- Vulnerability patterns differ across subregions, with eastern Sahel countries showing especially high population impact.
- The abstract emphasizes that urbanization and livelihood structure help explain why similar hazards produce different consequences across regions.

## Implementation implications

- The paper supports the wiki's broader argument that disaster risk is shaped by exposure and vulnerability patterns, not hazard labels alone.
